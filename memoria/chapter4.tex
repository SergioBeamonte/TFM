\chapter{Experimental Study}
\label{ch:experiments}

This chapter evaluates the pipeline of Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation} on two real influence diagrams of clinical origin. The
\emph{bypass} diagram, already introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:bypass-example} as the running example of the
implementation, plays the role of a small sanity-check benchmark. The
\emph{NHLy} diagram, presented in the next section, is one order of
magnitude larger and constitutes the main benchmark of the empirical study.

\section{Experimental protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}
% ==============================================================

The goal of the recovery is operational: to reproduce the
\emph{decisions} the expert would take. Everything in this chapter is
measured towards that goal. As mentioned previously, the rule bases are \emph{synthetic}
(generated by evaluating the original diagram, as described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation}) and then, the ground-truth model is known, so
the recovery can be judged quantitatively. Here, several important qualifications must be made. Not all recovered models are equally informative: rule bases do not fully determine every component of an influence diagram (solution space is highly degenerated), and different recovery methods vary in their ability to reconstruct the underlying model depending on the initialization and the given rules. Consequently, this dictates the hierarchy of the metrics below.

\subsection{Experiment metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

Each run is summarised by a small set of quantities, ordered here by how
much they matter for the recovery problem. They are computed on the best
individual of a run and aggregated over the independent repetitions with different rules and random initialization.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Rule accuracy (the primary measure).} The fraction of the
        \emph{whole} rule base that the recovered policy reproduces (equivalently $1-\mathcal{L}$ for the $0$--$1$ loss of
        Definition~\ref{def:cost}). Reproducing the expert's decisions is the actual objective, and in a real elicitation it is the only signal available. We report its \textbf{mean} over the
        repetitions.
  \item \textbf{Dispersion of the accuracy (reliability).} EDAs are stochastic methods; therefore, their quality depends not only on their performance but also on their repeatability and robustness to variations in the rule base and parameter initialization: one
        that swings between $80\%$ and $100\%$ across seeds is worse than
        one that sits steadily at $95\%$. We therefore report the
        \textbf{standard deviation} of the accuracy alongside its mean.
  \item \textbf{Recovery time (cost).} How expensive it is to reach that
        accuracy: the number of generations to convergence and the CPU
        time, measured as process CPU seconds rather
        than wall-clock, so that optimizers (EDAs) with very different
        per-generation cost are compared fairly. This metric becomes particularly important for large models, where recovery time can increase dramatically with the number of parameters.

        
  \item \textbf{Probability reconstruction (a secondary objective).} The
        mean squared error of the recovered conditional probability
        tables against the ground truth, $\mathrm{MSE}_{\text{CPT}}$.
        Unlike the rules, this rewards recovering the \emph{actual}
        probabilities, which are objective quantities with a true target;
        doing so is a stronger, desirable outcome, but it is subordinate
        to reproducing the rules and only meaningful where the chance
        model is itself optimized (in most experiments with NHLy, only utilities will be recovered, leaving appart probabilities, to reduce the computational cost).
%   \item \textbf{Utility distance (a diagnostic only, never a score).} The
%         distance between the recovered and the true utility is
%         \emph{not} used to rank anything. The optimal policy is invariant
%         under any transformation of the utility that preserves the
%         $\arg\max$ of the expected utility --- a positive affine rescaling
%         and, more generally, the whole region in which no decision flips
%         --- so infinitely many utility vectors reproduce exactly the same
%         rules. A perfectly correct recovery can thus sit arbitrarily far
%         from the ground-truth numbers. We report this distance only to
%         \emph{illustrate} the degeneracy of
%         Chapter~\ref{ch:framework}, never as a measure of quality.
\end{enumerate}

The ordering is deliberate. Quality is judged first by \emph{how many
rules} are reproduced and \emph{how reliably}, then by \emph{how
cheaply}; recovering the probability tables is a welcome bonus.
% ; and the
% utility numbers are explicitly removed from the scoreboard because the
% problem does not identify them.

\subsection{Several repetitions}
\label{sec:repetitions}

An EDA recovery is stochastic in two distinct ways, so a single run is a sample
of a random variable rather than a fixed measurement. First, the optimizer is
itself randomized: it starts from a random initial model (individuals drawn
from a uniform distribution) and at each generation re-estimates the model
from the highest-accuracy subset of the population before sampling the next one,
so even on the very same rules two runs may follow different trajectories and may
settle on different policies (as Section~\ref{sec:stopmode} examines in detail).
Second, \emph{which} rules are exposed is itself a random draw: each run trains
on a given fraction of the rule base, and the particular subset of decision
rules shown changes what can be identified, since different subsets constrain
the policy to different degrees. Reporting a single run would therefore conflate
the quality of a method with the luck of one seed. To turn the metrics of
Section~\ref{sec:metrics} into meaningful estimates, we run every configuration
several independent times, each with a different random seed that simultaneously
redraws the initialization and the rule sample: the \textbf{mean} over
repetitions estimates the expected accuracy (the primary metric) and their
\textbf{standard deviation} estimates the reliability (the second metric),
neither of which is computable from a single run.

It is worth noting that, in a real-world application, it would be the expert who
decides which rules to prioritize, according to their importance and how well
they capture the most critical decisions.

\subsection{Loss functions under study}
\label{sec:losses}

The recovery minimizes the aggregated per-rule loss of
Definition~\ref{def:fitness}; the experiments in this chapter sweep its five
instances, which differ only in \emph{how} they penalize for each rule the candidate
gets wrong. They are not redundant, they shape the different strategies, and the purpose of comparing them is to find out whether a
smoother, more informative signal helps the EDA to converge. Briefly:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\texttt{binary}} (the $0$--$1$ loss). Counts how many training
        rules the candidate fails to reproduce, scoring nothing for
        \emph{how} wrong each one is. It is the strictest criterion and the one that most faithfully reflects the actual objective (rule accuracy), but it yields a loss landscape with many flat regions, which can make it more difficult to converge to local optima. We test it as the natural baseline and the one all
        other losses must beat to be worth their extra complexity.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{regret}} and \textbf{\texttt{margin}}. Instead of a
        yes/no verdict they measure \emph{by how much} the utility of the
        chosen action falls short of the best one, turning the landscape
        continuous and giving the optimizer a direction to descend; \texttt{margin}
        additionally demands a safety gap, rewarding decisions that win
        \emph{clearly} rather than by a little. We test them to see whether this
        smoother signal accelerates or stabilizes
        convergence relative to \texttt{binary}.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{softmax}} and \textbf{\texttt{entropy}}. These score the
        log-likelihood of the expert action under a softmax over the candidate
        utilities; \texttt{entropy}
        adds a regularizer that discourages over-confident utility vectors. We
        test them to check whether the confidence level improves the recovered model.
\end{itemize}

All five are crossed against the optimizers in the grid of
Section~\ref{sec:grid1}; the preliminary study below will fix \texttt{binary} because it is the simplest and the easiest to evaluate.


% ==============================================================
\section{Convergence and the stopping criterion}
\label{sec:stopmode}
% ==============================================================

Prior to conducting a full sweep of the hyperparameter and configuration grid, we performed a preliminary study. This study aims to solve, or at least hint at the solutions for, two linked questions that are kept fixed throughout the grid: \emph{when a run should stop}, and \emph{what does converge mean} in the context of a recovery problem.

With the \texttt{binary} fitness, an individual computes 0 penalty score when it reproduces \emph{every} training rule it was shown; we call such individuals \textbf{satisfiers}. A natural stopping rule
is therefore to wait until a given fraction of the population has become a
satisfier: the \texttt{top}$X$ criterion interrupts the run once $X\%$ of the
$50$ individuals satisfy all the training rules. We track the thresholds
$X\in\{50,70,90,95,100\}$ together with the four EDA variants (UMDA, EMNA, EGNA
and KEDA on \emph{bypass}; only the cheap UMDA and EMNA on the costly
\emph{NHLy}, where the multivariate EGNA and the kernel-based KEDA are
prohibitively expensive) and the \texttt{binary}
fitness, exposing $5$, $10$, $20$ and $30\%$ of the decision-rule base. The two
benchmarks differ in what is recovered and in how many rules those percentages represent. Besides, on \emph{bypass} the recovery optimizes \emph{both} the conditional
probability tables and the utilities (\texttt{both} mode) and each configuration is repeated $10$ times; its rule base has $20$ decision rules, so the four fractions correspond to $1$, $2$, $4$ and $6$ rules. On \emph{NHLy}, whose size makes a full recovery expensive, \emph{only} the utilities are
optimized (\texttt{utility\_only} mode, leaving the probability tables
untouched) and each configuration is repeated $5$ times; its $67$ decision
rules turn the same fractions into $3$, $7$, $13$ and $20$ rules. Each run is
capped at $100$ generations with at least $50$ so that the convergence curves
show enough evolution, and each repetition draws a fresh random rule sample and
initialization (different $\texttt{seed}$ in random function).

The crucial point is that satisfying the training rules does \emph{not} fix the complete optimal policy. Many individuals achieve a perfect, zero-loss, fit to the
rules they were shown and yet disagree on the rules they were
\emph{not} shown (due to the degeneracy explained in
Chapter~\ref{ch:framework}). To expose it we measure, their accuracy on the \textbf{whole} rule base, and report its mean together with the best and the worst satisfier.

\subsection{The stabilization level}
\label{sec:stopmode-init}

Figure~\ref{fig:stopmode-reps} shows ten repetitions of a single
configuration (UMDA, \texttt{binary}, $30\%$ of the rules) together with their
mean. \textbf{Every repetition stabilizes} (as the nature of an EDA guarantees):
after the first generations each trajectory reaches a plateau and stays on it,
so the method is well behaved on all seeds. What changes from one run to another
is not \emph{whether} it settles but the \emph{level} at which it does. In this
particular configuration most repetitions stabilize on a $75$--$85\%$ plateau,
but at least one settles much lower, around $50\%$ --- not a failure to
converge, but convergence to a policy no better than the one obtained with no
rules at all --- so the across-repetition mean sits around $72\%$, below the
better individual runs. Because each repetition starts from a different random
initialization and sees a different rule sample, \textbf{the outcome of a single
run is itself a random variable}: the same optimizer, fitness and rule budget
can stabilize on a good policy or on a mediocre one depending only on where it
started. The markers \texttt{top50}/\texttt{70}/\texttt{90}/\texttt{95}/\texttt{100}
on the mean curve give the median generation at which each stopping threshold is
reached; that they cluster within a narrow band of generations already
anticipates the next result.

\subsection{The stopping threshold: accuracy versus cost}
\label{sec:stopmode-cost}

The stopping criterion controls \emph{when} to halt, and through that the cost
of a run. It does not change \emph{what} the search does: with a fixed seed the
EDA visits the same sequence of populations, and a stricter threshold merely
reads that trajectory out at a later generation. The question is therefore
purely one of trade-off --- \emph{how much accuracy a later stop buys for the
extra generations it costs}. Figures~\ref{fig:stopmode-ex-bypass}
and~\ref{fig:stopmode-ex-nhlv1} make it visible on one configuration of each
network: the thick curve is the across-run mean accuracy and the markers
\texttt{top50}--\texttt{top100} sit at the median generation where each
threshold fires, i.e.\ where $50$--$100\%$ of the population have become
satisfiers.

The two example configurations already show that the answer is
network-dependent. On \emph{bypass} (Figure~\ref{fig:stopmode-ex-bypass}) the
accuracy rises steeply for the first ten generations and then \textbf{flattens
into a plateau}, so once the knee is passed the threshold barely matters. On
\emph{NHLy} (Figure~\ref{fig:stopmode-ex-nhlv1}) convergence is \textbf{slower
and the curve keeps climbing} well beyond \texttt{top50}: there a stricter
threshold genuinely buys accuracy, but every increment costs many generations of
an already expensive run, and the strictest thresholds are often \emph{never
reached} within the budget (the \texttt{top100} marker is absent). A single
example therefore cannot fix the criterion; the trade-off has to be read across
all configurations at once.

Figure~\ref{fig:stopmode-tradeoff} does exactly that. Sweeping the threshold
from $10\%$ to $100\%$ and averaging over every optimizer, rule fraction and
repetition, it plots the recovered accuracy against the cost (generations) at
which each threshold fires, and annotates how reliably each threshold is reached.
The curve has the canonical diminishing-returns shape: accuracy climbs fast,
bends at a knee, then crawls. \textbf{We adopt \texttt{top90}} as that knee --- the
threshold that captures essentially all the achievable accuracy while remaining
reliably attainable. At \texttt{top90} the population mean reaches $67\%$ on
\emph{bypass} (generation~$17$, reached in $99\%$ of runs) and $81\%$ on
\emph{NHLy} (generation~$29$, reached in $88\%$): on \emph{bypass} it sits right
on the plateau, and on \emph{NHLy} it captures the bulk of a still-rising curve.
Going further is a poor bargain on both counts: \texttt{top95} and
\texttt{top100} add only a point or two for a steep jump in generations and ---
decisively --- are reached in barely half of the \emph{NHLy} runs, so as a
\emph{stopping rule} they would usually never fire and the run would just exhaust
its generation budget. Stopping earlier (\texttt{top50}) would instead leave
several points on the table precisely on \emph{NHLy}, the network we care about,
where the curve is still rising at that point. \texttt{top90} is thus the
strictest threshold that is still robustly reachable --- exactly the
\emph{good enough} point we want --- and we fix it for the rest of the study.

Two reasons let us reuse this single criterion throughout rather than re-tuning
it per experiment. First, \texttt{top90} is not tied to the \texttt{binary}
notion of a satisfier: in general it fires when ``the best $90\%$ of the
population have reached the \emph{target quality} of the loss in use'', and that
target is defined per loss (zero penalty for \texttt{binary}, the corresponding
log-likelihood for \texttt{softmax}/\texttt{entropy}, the prescribed margin for
\texttt{regret}/\texttt{margin}). The rule therefore means the same thing ---
``most of the population has converged'' --- whatever objective is optimized.
Second, the shape that justifies the choice, a steep rise into a plateau, is a
property of the \emph{EDA dynamics} on this problem, not of any particular loss;
the loss only reshapes the landscape the search climbs. The grid of
Section~\ref{sec:grid1} confirms this empirically --- the choice of loss changes
the outcome far less than the choice of optimizer --- so a threshold calibrated
with \texttt{binary} transfers safely to the other four losses, which is why the
temperature-and-fitness study fixes \texttt{top90} for all of them.

\subsection{Results: degeneracy and the role of the optimizer}
\label{sec:stopmode-degeneracy}

The substantive finding concerns the satisfiers themselves: individuals that all
reproduce the training rules \emph{perfectly} still scatter widely on the
\emph{whole} rule base. Table~\ref{tab:stopmode-satisfiers} quantifies this for
every optimizer, and the convergence grids of
Figures~\ref{fig:stopmode-bypass} and~\ref{fig:stopmode-nhlv1} show the same
story over generations --- the gap between the green ``best individual'' and the
red ``worst individual'' is the live degeneracy. Two regularities hold across
all four optimizers and both networks. First, \textbf{satisfying the training
rules does not pin down the policy}: at $5\%$ of the rules the best and worst
satisfier can differ by $30$--$70$ points while remaining indistinguishable to
the training objective. Second, \textbf{adding rules collapses the degeneracy}:
every spread in the table shrinks monotonically as the rule fraction grows, and
the worst satisfier climbs steadily towards the best. More constraints carve
away the directions along which the policy is free to differ, pulling the
satisfier set towards the true model --- the empirical counterpart of the
identifiability argument of Chapter~\ref{ch:framework}: the rules are what
identify the policy, and the more of them are seen, the less room is left for a
penalty-free individual to be wrong.

\subsection{Why the band widens: a Markov-chain reading}
\label{sec:stopmode-markov}

A striking feature of Figures~\ref{fig:stopmode-bypass}
and~\ref{fig:stopmode-nhlv1} is that the $\pm1\sigma$ band is not constant: it
is almost invisible at the first generation and fans out as the run proceeds.
The numbers confirm the eye --- on \emph{bypass} at $30\%$ of the rules the
across-repetition standard deviation of the mean accuracy grows from
$\sim\!2$ points at generation~$1$ to $\sim\!6$--$10$ points by generation~$10$
and then roughly freezes, fluctuating around that level for the rest of the
run. The band therefore \emph{accumulates} during the transient and
\emph{saturates} afterwards; it does not keep growing without bound.

This behaviour is exactly what a Markovian reading of the EDA predicts, so the
intuition of a random-walk-like movement is essentially correct. A recovery run
is a Markov chain on the space of probabilistic models: the model
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1}$ of the next generation is obtained from
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_t$ alone --- sample $50$ individuals, keep the selected
ones, re-estimate the marginals --- with no memory of how $\boldsymbol{\theta}_t$
was reached. Each repetition is thus an independent realization of the
\emph{same} stochastic recursion, and at generation~$1$ they all start from the
same near-uniform model, which is why every run sits at $\approx\!50\%$ (bypass)
or $\approx\!42\%$ (NHLy) and the band is negligible. From there, two
independent realizations of a stochastic recursion drift apart: the finite
sampling of the population and the particular random rule subset each run was
given perturb the early steps, and those perturbations are propagated and
compounded generation after generation, so the across-run variance accumulates
just as the variance of a random walk grows with time. Critically, the
recursion has no single attractor towards which the chains could reconverge: by
the degeneracy of Section~\ref{sec:stopmode-degeneracy} the loss has a whole set
of distinct optima (the satisfiers), so different chains are pulled towards
different fixed points and the spread keeps opening instead of closing.

What stops the band from widening forever is the other half of the Markov
picture. Selection drives towards $0$ and $1$ the marginals of the parameters
that the training rules \emph{constrain} --- those that decide whether an
individual is a satisfier --- so along that subspace the chain freezes quickly
and every run agrees; the parameters left \emph{free} by the degeneracy of
Section~\ref{sec:stopmode-degeneracy} face an almost flat fitness and are hardly
moved by selection. Once each run has fanned out towards its own basin, the
across-run spread of the trajectories stops growing, which is why the band
accumulates during the transient and then plateaus instead of diverging.

A natural further question --- and the right way to read the best- and
worst-individual curves --- is whether the individuals \emph{within} a single
run eventually converge to a common solution. They do, but slowly, and here the
$100$-generation cap stops the runs before the process completes, so the
populations we observe are still \emph{contracting} clouds rather than finished
points. The within-run best$-$worst gap falls throughout the run: on \emph{NHLy}
at $30\%$ of the rules it drops from $\sim\!74$ points to $\sim\!5$ under UMDA,
which all but converges to a single --- if mediocre --- solution by
generation~$20$, and from $\sim\!74$ to $\sim\!11$ under EMNA, still gently
descending at the last generation. EMNA contracts far more slowly because its
covariance model preserves diversity longer, but the tendency is the same, and
it is the familiar drift of a finite population reinforced by the covariance
contraction of a Gaussian EDA: even along the flat degenerate directions a
population of $50$ loses variance generation after generation, so with enough
budget each run would collapse to essentially one point of the optimum set. The
within-run spread we still see is therefore a finite-budget effect, not a
property of the limit.

What does \emph{not} disappear with more generations is the dispersion
\emph{across} runs. Different repetitions drift towards different points of the
degenerate set, and letting them converge internally only sharpens each into its
own distinct solution without bringing the solutions any closer together. The
$\pm1\sigma$ band therefore survives in the long run --- its width measures the
spread of the per-run limit points, that is, the satisfier degeneracy of
Section~\ref{sec:stopmode-degeneracy} read across runs --- and it shrinks only
when the set of reachable optima itself shrinks, through more rules or a more
contractive optimizer such as UMDA. In short, the growing shadow is the
spreading marginal of a Markov chain whose individual realizations each
collapse, slowly, onto \emph{different} points of a degenerate optimum set, and
its asymptotic size measures how degenerate the recovery problem is.

Two practical conclusions carry over to the grids that follow. First, the
stopping criterion trades cost for quality with sharply diminishing returns, so
a moderate threshold is enough: we fix \texttt{top90}, the knee that captures
essentially all the achievable accuracy while staying reliably reachable
(Section~\ref{sec:stopmode-cost}). Second, and more
importantly, \emph{reproducing the training rules is not the same as
recovering the policy}: the gap between the two is the satisfier spread, it is
real, and the only reliable way to close it is to expose more rules ---
precisely the trend the grid experiments quantify next.


\section{Grid experiment 1}
\label{sec:grid1}

The first experiment is a systematic grid search on the \emph{bypass}
benchmark, used here as a controlled sanity check before moving to the
larger NHLy diagram. The grid crosses the four factors that define a recovery
run: the \textbf{EDA variant} (UMDA, EMNA, EGNA and KEDA), the
\textbf{fitness function} (\texttt{binary}, \texttt{margin}, \texttt{softmax},
\texttt{regret} and \texttt{entropy}), the \textbf{fraction of the rule base}
exposed to the optimizer ($5$, $10$, $20$ and $40\%$ of the decision rules)
and the \textbf{sampling mode} (symmetric and non-symmetric). All remaining
parameters are fixed: both chance and utility temperatures at $T=1$, the
\texttt{top90} stopping criterion, a population of $50$ individuals
($400$ for KEDA, whose kernel-density estimate degenerates with fewer
samples than variables), at most $60$ generations and $5$ independent
repetitions per configuration. Because the bypass diagram is small, this
experiment optimizes \emph{both} the conditional probability tables and the
utilities, so the probability-reconstruction metric is meaningful here. The
crossing yields $160$ configurations, which we analyse following the metric
hierarchy of Section~\ref{sec:metrics}: first accuracy, then its dispersion
and cost, and finally probability reconstruction.

\subsection{Rule accuracy}
\label{sec:grid1-accuracy}

Figure~\ref{fig:grid1-accuracy} shows the convergence of the
population-average accuracy along the generations, with one panel per
combination of fitness function (rows) and fraction of decision rules
(columns) and one curve per optimizer (averaged over the two sampling modes,
whose effect turns out to be negligible). The summary numbers quoted below
refer to the accuracy of the \emph{best} individual at convergence, as
defined in Section~\ref{sec:metrics}.

Three regularities emerge. First, \textbf{the optimizer is the dominant
factor}: KEDA recovers the network almost perfectly ($96.9\%$ average
accuracy, reaching the exact $100\%$ in several configurations), whereas
UMDA, EMNA and EGNA plateau around $86$--$88\%$. The gap between KEDA and the
rest is larger than the effect of any other factor in the grid. Second,
\textbf{accuracy grows monotonically with the amount of rules}, from
$85.8\%$ at $5\%$ of the rule base to $93.7\%$ at $40\%$; the gain is steep
for the weaker optimizers --- EGNA climbs about eight points across the range
--- and almost flat for KEDA, which already starts near $93\%$ at $5\%$ and
saturates close to $100\%$. In other words, \emph{more rules can compensate
for a weaker optimizer, but a strong optimizer needs few rules to get far}.
Third, \textbf{the fitness function barely matters}: the five functions lie
within $1.5$ points of each other ($88.8$--$90.3\%$ on average), and the
simplest one, \texttt{binary}, is essentially as good as the rest, so there
is no incentive to complicate the objective. The sampling mode is likewise
immaterial ($90.0\%$ symmetric versus $89.4\%$ non-symmetric). The panels
also show that convergence is fast and scales with the amount of rules: runs
stabilise within about half a dozen generations at $5\%$ of the rules and up
to roughly forty at $40\%$, and KEDA's advantage comes from a larger
population rather than from more generations.

\subsection{Reliability and recovery cost}
\label{sec:grid1-std-cpu}

Mean accuracy alone does not tell the whole story: a stochastic method that
swings across seeds is less useful than one that lands consistently on the
same value, and a method is only practical if its cost is acceptable.
Figure~\ref{fig:grid1-std-cpu} reports both, the standard deviation of the
accuracy across repetitions (reliability) and the total process CPU time
(cost), broken down by optimizer and fraction of rules.

The reliability picture reinforces the accuracy ranking: \textbf{KEDA is also
the most stable optimizer}, with a dispersion that falls from
$\sim\!3.5$ points at $5\%$ of the rules to $\sim\!1.1$ at $40\%$ (and
exactly $0$ in the configurations that always reach $100\%$), while the other
three remain noisy throughout, in the $5$--$6.5$ point range almost
independently of the amount of rules. Adding rules therefore not only raises
KEDA's accuracy but tightens it, whereas it does little for the dispersion of
UMDA, EMNA or EGNA.

The cost picture splits the optimizers into two families. \textbf{UMDA and
EMNA are an order of magnitude cheaper} than the rest ($\sim\!0.5$\,s per run
at the largest setting), whereas \textbf{KEDA and EGNA are expensive}
($\sim\!8$--$10\,$s), the price KEDA pays for its large population and the
kernel-density estimation. The two views together identify EGNA as the worst
trade-off in the grid --- it is as expensive as KEDA but as inaccurate and
noisy as the cheap pair --- and frame the practical choice as a clean
dilemma: KEDA when accuracy and reliability are paramount, or UMDA/EMNA when
CPU budget is the binding constraint and an accuracy ceiling of $\sim\!92$--$93\%$
at $40\%$ of the rules is acceptable.

\subsection{Probability reconstruction}
\label{sec:grid1-mse}

Finally we examine the secondary objective: how well the recovered
conditional probability tables match the ground truth, measured by
$\mathrm{MSE}_{\text{CPT}}$. Because the bypass experiment optimizes the
chance model as well as the utilities, this quantity is informative here.
Figure~\ref{fig:grid1-mse} relates it to the rule accuracy (left) and breaks
it down by optimizer and fraction of rules (right).

\iffalse % figura comentada (imagen borrada)
\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/grid1_mse.png}
\caption{Probability reconstruction on the \emph{bypass} benchmark.
(a) CPT reconstruction error against rule accuracy; each point is one

\label{fig:grid1-mse}
\end{figure}

The result is the empirical counterpart of the degeneracy discussed in
Chapter~\ref{ch:framework}: \textbf{reproducing the decisions does not
reconstruct the probabilities}. The two metrics are essentially uncorrelated
(Pearson $r=0.15$), and the configurations that reach $99$--$100\%$ rule
accuracy still carry a substantial CPT error ($\mathrm{MSE}_{\text{CPT}}$
between $0.73$ and $0.96$), no lower than that of far less accurate runs. The
breakdown is, if anything, counter-intuitive: \textbf{exposing more rules
increases the CPT error} for every optimizer except UMDA, because the extra
rules push the search to fit the policy more aggressively, dragging the
probability tables further from their true values along directions that leave
the $\arg\max$ of the expected utility --- and hence the rules --- unchanged.
Notably, UMDA attains both the lowest and the flattest CPT error
($\sim\!0.66$ on average) despite its mediocre accuracy, whereas EGNA pairs
the worst accuracy with the worst probability reconstruction. The reading is
unambiguous: rule accuracy and probability fidelity are distinct, weakly
related objectives, and a high score on the primary metric is no guarantee of
a faithful chance model. This justifies treating probability reconstruction
as a subordinate, ``bonus'' criterion rather than a target, exactly as the
metric hierarchy of Section~\ref{sec:metrics} prescribes.


\section{Grid experiment 2: the NHLy diagram}
\label{sec:grid2}

The second experiment moves to the \emph{NHLy} diagram, the main benchmark of
the study and roughly two orders of magnitude larger than \emph{bypass}
($67$ decision rules and a parameter vector of several thousand entries, see
Table~\ref{tab:benchmark-sizes}). The purpose is to test whether the
conclusions of the small benchmark survive at scale. Two design choices
follow from the size of the network. First, to keep the cost manageable, the
recovery is run in \texttt{utility\_only} mode: only the utility function is
optimized while the conditional probability tables are left untouched.
Consequently \textbf{the probability-reconstruction metric does not apply to
this experiment} ($\mathrm{MSE}_{\text{CPT}}=0$ by construction), and the
analysis is restricted to accuracy, reliability and cost. Second, because a
full $160$-cell grid at NHLy prices would be prohibitively expensive, only
UMDA was swept over the complete grid; EMNA and KEDA were evaluated on a
representative subset (the \texttt{binary} and \texttt{regret} fitnesses, at

estimate degenerates for a parameter vector this large, returning no usable

scalable choice}, combining the highest accuracy, a dispersion that shrinks
with the rule budget, and a moderate cost. The qualitative regularities of
\emph{bypass} carry over (more rules help, the fitness function and the
sampling mode are secondary), but the quantitative winner does not, which
argues for selecting the EDA variant per network rather than fixing it
